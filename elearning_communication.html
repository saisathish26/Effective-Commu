Module 2 — Active Listening

Listen to Understand,
Not to Reply

Active listening isn't passive silence — it's an intentional practice. Click each level to see what it looks like in practice.

L1
Pay Full Attention
Put your phone face-down. Close unneeded tabs. Make eye contact. Signal with your body that this conversation matters. Research shows undivided attention increases information retention by 60%.
L2
Withhold Judgment
Let the speaker finish before forming a response. Notice when you're mentally drafting a reply — that's the moment to refocus on their words. The pause between listening and responding is where understanding lives.
L3
Reflect & Clarify
Paraphrase back: "So what I'm hearing is..." This confirms understanding AND signals respect. Ask one clarifying question — not five. Depth over breadth.
L4
Acknowledge Emotion
Name what you sense: "That sounds frustrating." This validates the person before you solve the problem. People who feel heard are 4x more likely to accept difficult news constructively.
Communication Impact
Verbal Words 7%
Tone of Voice 38%
Body Language 55%

Mehrabian's model: when tone and words conflict, listeners rely primarily on nonverbal signals to judge meaning.

Key Takeaway

93% of communication is delivered through how you say something — not the words you choose. Mastering active listening means tuning into the full signal.

Module 2 — Scenario Check

What Would You Do?

Situation: During a team meeting, your colleague Maya starts sharing concerns about the new project timeline. While she's talking, you realize you have a strong rebuttal ready. What do you do?
A
Interrupt Maya with your rebuttal to keep the meeting on schedule.
B
Start drafting your response mentally while Maya is still talking.
C
Let Maya finish, then ask one clarifying question before sharing your perspective.
D
Wait for Maya to finish, then immediately deliver your prepared rebuttal.
✅ Excellent! Letting Maya finish and asking a clarifying question first demonstrates active listening. It ensures you fully understand her concern before responding — and makes Maya feel heard, which increases receptivity to your perspective.
❌ Not quite. This approach prioritizes your response over understanding Maya's concern. Even well-intentioned interruptions or premature rebuttals signal that you've stopped listening — which often escalates tension rather than resolving it.
Module 3 — Clarity & Structure

Structure Your
Message to Land

Clear communication isn't about saying more — it's about saying the right things in the right order. Use the PREP framework for any high-stakes message.

📌
P — Point
↗ Click to reveal
Lead with your main point. Don't bury the lede. "I recommend we delay the launch by two weeks." State it first, justify it after.
🔍
R — Reason
↗ Click to reveal
Give the one most compelling reason. Not five. One strong reason is more persuasive than a list. "Because QA found 3 critical bugs that affect checkout."
📊
E — Evidence
↗ Click to reveal
Support with one concrete data point or example. "In the last launch with similar bugs, we saw a 34% cart abandonment spike in week one."
🔁
P — Point (Again)
↗ Click to reveal
Circle back to your point, now with a call to action. "So I'd like us to agree on a two-week buffer today." This creates a memorable, action-oriented close.
Communication Principles
✂️
Edit Ruthlessly
If your email is over 5 sentences, it needs editing — not more sentences. Ask: "Can I cut 30% without losing meaning?" Usually, yes.
🎯
One Ask Per Message
Multiple requests dilute urgency. "Can you review the doc AND confirm the meeting AND update the tracker?" → One gets done. One ask per message.
📅
Specify Deadlines
"ASAP" is not a deadline. "By Thursday 5pm" is. Vague timelines create anxiety and missed expectations — specific ones create accountability.

Key Nonverbal Signals
👁️
Eye Contact
Aim for 60–70% eye contact when speaking. Too little signals insecurity; too much can feel confrontational. In video calls, look at the camera — not the screen.
🤲
Open Posture
Uncrossed arms, slight forward lean, and open palms signal receptivity. These cues are processed subconsciously — before you say a word.
🎙️
Pace & Pause
Slow down when making key points. Strategic pauses signal confidence and give listeners time to absorb information. Rushing signals anxiety.

Module 5 — Difficult Conversations

The SBI Framework

Situation–Behavior–Impact (SBI) is the gold standard for delivering feedback and navigating tough conversations. Click each element to see it in action.

📍
S — Situation
↗ Click to reveal
"During yesterday's client call..."

Anchor the conversation to a specific time and place. Avoid "you always" or "you never" — these trigger defensiveness. Specificity signals fairness.
👁️
B — Behavior
↗ Click to reveal
"...you interrupted the client three times while they were explaining their concern..."

Describe observable behavior only — not intent, not personality. "You were rude" vs. "You interrupted" — one is a label, one is a fact.
💥
I — Impact
↗ Click to reveal
"...and the client mentioned feeling unheard. They haven't responded to our follow-up since."

Connect behavior to real consequences — for the team, the client, or the organization. Impact makes the conversation real and removes the sense of personal attack.
🚫
Common Traps
↗ Click to reveal
Avoid the "feedback sandwich" — good/bad/good signals insincerity. Don't delay hard conversations until reviews. Don't give feedback when either person is emotionally flooded. Timing matters as much as technique.
🔁
Receiving Feedback
↗ Click to reveal
Receiving feedback is a skill too. Default to "thank you" before defending. Ask: "Can you give me a specific example?" Sleep on strong emotional reactions before responding. Growth lives on the other side of discomfort.
⚡
Psychological Safety
↗ Click to reveal
The best communication happens in psychologically safe environments. As a leader or peer, you set the tone: normalize questions, celebrate admitting mistakes, and never punish candor.
Module 5 — Final Scenario

Apply What
You've Learned

Situation: Your manager sends a Slack message: "The report you sent was not what I asked for." No further context. You feel defensive and hurt. Using SBI, how should you respond?
A
Reply immediately: "I followed your exact instructions — here's the email thread."
B
Step away briefly, then respond: "I want to make sure I get this right. Can we connect for 10 minutes so I understand what was missing?"
C
Forward the original brief to HR and CC your manager to establish a record.
D
Send a detailed rebuttal explaining each section of the report and your reasoning.
✅ Perfect. You regulated your emotions first. Then you responded with curiosity — not defensiveness. Requesting a quick sync opens dialogue and gathers the context you need to actually fix the issue. This is SBI in reverse: you're creating a safe space for your manager to specify the Behavior and Impact.
❌ Think again. Reacting from an emotional state — or escalating immediately — closes dialogue and damages trust. The goal is understanding, not winning. What information do you actually need to move forward?
